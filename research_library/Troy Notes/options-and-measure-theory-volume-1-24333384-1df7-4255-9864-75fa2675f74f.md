\delta_{1}, \delta_{2}, \ldots, \delta_{n}
$$

at times

$$
t<t_{1}<t_{2}<\cdots<t_{n}<T .
$$

The asset price $S_{t}$ will be reduced by all future dividendend payments. These payments are discounted by the risk-free interest evaluted at the time of payment. For the i'th dividend payment
the asset price is

$$
S_{t}-\delta_{i} e^{-r\left(t_{i}-t\right)}
$$

It follows that for all dividend payments the asset price at time $t$ is

$$
S_{t}-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \delta_{i} e^{-r\left(t_{i}-t\right)}
$$

In Problem 9 it was shown that under the no-arbitrage condion an no dividends that

$$
F(t, T)=S_{t} e^{r(T-t)}
$$

It follows that for $n$ dividend payments

$$
F(t, T)=\left[S_{t}-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \delta_{i} e^{-r\left(t_{i}-t\right)}\right] e^{r(T-t)}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& =S_{t} e^{r(T-t)}-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \delta_{i} e^{-r\left(t_{i}-t\right)} e^{r(T-t)} \\
& =S_{t} e^{r(T-t)}-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \delta_{i} e^{r\left(T-t_{i}\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

which is the desired result

$$

## solution

Start with a discrete solution with $n$ time intervals and then take the limit $n \rightarrow \infty$ to obtain the continuous limit.
The discretized interval $[t, T]$ is given by

$$
t_{i}=t+\frac{c(T-t)}{n} \quad i=0,1,2, \ldots, n
$$

note that $t_{0}=t$ and $t_{n}=T$. Let the dividerd payment at $t_{i}$ be

$$
\frac{D(T-t)}{n} A_{t_{i}}
$$

Since all dividends are reinvested the number of shares after the first dividend payment is

$$
\begin{aligned}
A_{t_{1}} & =A_{t_{0}}\left[1+\frac{D\left(\frac{T-t}{n}\right)}{n}\right] \\
& =A_{t}\left[1+\frac{D\left(\frac{T-t}{n}\right)}{n}\right]
\end{aligned}
$$

and at the next posment

$$
\begin{aligned}
A_{t_{2}} & =A_{t_{1}}\left[1+\frac{D(T-t)}{n}\right] \\
& =A_{t}\left[1+\frac{D(T-t)}{n}\right]^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

It follows that after $n$ payments

$$
A_{t n}=A_{t}\left[1+\frac{D(T-t)}{n}\right]^{n}
$$

The continuous limit is
obtained in the limit

$$
A_{T}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} A_{t}\left[1+\frac{D(T-t)}{n}\right]^{n}
$$

using

$$
e^{x}=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty}\left[1+\frac{x}{n}\right]^{n}
$$

gives

$$
A_{\tau}=A_{t} e^{D(T-t)}
$$

Thus if at time $t$ one share is held at time $T A_{t} e^{\Delta(T-t)}$ shares will be held.

If we start by buying $e^{-D(T-t)}$ shares the number of shares at time $T$ will be one. It follows that the total value of the stock at time $t$ is

$$
S_{t} e^{-D(T-t)}
$$

Recall that in problem $q$ it
was shown that under the the assumption of no-arbitrage opportunity and no dividends that the future contract price is.

$$
F(t, T)=S_{t} e^{r(T-t)}
$$

Thus conder the assumption of continuous duidend yied,

which is the desired result.

## Problem 2: Covered Put

A covered put investment strategy is implemented by short selling an asset and then selling an OTM put option on the same asset

Explain why a put writer would invest in a portfolio using this strategy. Show that this strategy is protitable when

$$
P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \geqslant K-S_{t}
$$

where $P\left(s_{t}, t, k_{1} T\right)$ is the price of the put option writen on the asset with strike price $k$ and expry time $T>t$.
Draw the profit diagram of this stretegy at expry time $T>t$.

## Solution

A put writer expects the oalve of the asset to decrease relative to the option strike price. The put writer increases his income from the premium recieved from buyers of the option. By short selling the asset the writer does not need to worry about decreases in the asset value. If the asset price increases significantly beyond the strike price the put writer will lose money.
Let $t<T$ denote the time the put contract is written and let $S_{t}$ dende the asset price at $t$.
The payoff for the portfolio is the sum of the short sell payoff and the short put payoff.

The short sell payoff is given by

$$
\psi_{T}^{S}=-S_{T}
$$

and the short put payoff is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
\psi_{T}^{P} & =\min \left(S_{T}-k, 0\right) \\
& =-\max \left(k-S_{T}, 0\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

The portfolio payoff is given by

$$
\psi_{T}=-S_{T}-\max \left(K-S_{T}, 0\right)
$$

for $S_{T} \geqslant K$

$$
\psi_{T}=-S_{T}
$$

and for $S_{T}<k$

$$
\psi_{T}=-k
$$

Thus the payoff is given
by

$$
u_{T}= \begin{cases}-S_{T} & S_{T} \geqslant K \\ -K & S_{T}<K\end{cases}
$$

The following payoff diagram shows the short sell, short put and covered put payoffs.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_21_a2ecc42d6609752f415bg-150.jpg?height=1107&width=1246&top_left_y=856&top_left_x=161)

The profit for the short sell position is given by

$$
r_{T}^{s}=S_{t}-S_{T}
$$

and the profit for the short put is given by

$$
\gamma_{\tau}^{P}=-\max \left(k-S_{T}, 0\right)+P\left(S_{t}, t ; k, \tau\right)
$$

It follows that the portfolio profit is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
\gamma_{T}= & \gamma_{T}^{s}+\gamma_{T}^{p} \\
= & s_{t}-s_{T}-\max \left(k-s_{T} 0\right) \\
& +P\left(s_{t, t} ; k, T\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus for $s_{t} \geqslant k$

$$
\gamma_{T}=S_{t}-S_{T}+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)
$$

and for $s_{t}<k$

$$
\gamma_{T}=S_{t}-S_{T}-\left(K-S_{T}\right)+P\left(S_{t}, t ; k, T\right)
$$

$$
=S_{t}-K+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)
$$

Thus the portditio profit is given by

$$
\gamma_{T}= \begin{cases}S_{t}-S_{T}+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) & S_{T} \geqslant K \\ S_{t}-K+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) & S_{T}<K\end{cases}
$$

Note that for $S_{T}<K$ the profit is independen of $S_{T}$ so is constant for $s_{T} \geqslant K$ the profit is a linearly decreasing function of $S_{T}$. It follows that the profit is maximum for $S_{T}<K$ for the portfolio to be profitable it must be that

$$
\begin{aligned}
& s_{t}-k+P\left(s_{t}, t ; k, T\right) \geqslant 0 \\
\Rightarrow & P\left(s_{t}, t, k, \tau\right) \geqslant k-s_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

If this condition is satisfied the maximum profit is
given by

$$
r_{\tau}^{+}=S_{t}-K+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, \tau\right)
$$

A bownds can also be placed or St using,

$$
\begin{aligned}
& S_{t}-K+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \geqslant 0 \\
\Rightarrow & S_{t} \geqslant K-P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

The portfolio is at break even when for $S_{T} \geqslant K$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \gamma_{T}=0 \\
\Rightarrow & S_{t}-S_{T}+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)=0 \\
\Rightarrow & S_{T}=S_{t}+P\left(S_{t}, t, K, T\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

In swmmary a covered put strategy is protitable only

$$
P\left(S_{t}, t, K, \tau\right) \geqslant K-S_{t}
$$

This relation also places a constraint on $S_{t}$,

$$
S_{t} \geqslant K-P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)
$$

The maximum profit is positive because of the previous constraint and occurs for $S_{T}<K$. It is given by

$$
r_{T}^{+}=s_{t}-K+P\left(s_{t}, t ; K, \tau\right)>0
$$

Portfolio break even occurs for $S_{T}>K$ and is given by

$$
S_{\tau}=S_{t}+P\left(S_{t}, t ; K, \tau\right)
$$

The portfolio has no minimum value so loses are not constrained but are unbounded.

## Problem 3: Protective Call

A protective call is an investment stratesy constructed by short selling and buying an OTM call option on the same stock.
Explain why a call hoder would set up this portfolio trading strategy and show that this strategy is profitable if

$$
C\left(S_{t, t}, K, T\right) \geqslant S_{t}-K
$$

where $C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)$ is the call option price written on the asset $S_{t}$ with strike price $k$ at time and expiry date $T>t$.

Draw the profit diagram of this statesy at expiry date $T>t$.

Solution

In a protective call strategy an investor taking a dront position in an asset insures himself from losses if the asset price increases beyord the strike price. A long call position on the same asset at the same strike price and expiry date would allow the investor to purchase the stock needed to cover the call position. If the asset price stays below the strike price the buyer of the call will only lose the premium pad for the call option.

Let $t<T$ denote the time the contracts are written and let $S_{t}$ dende the asset price at $t$.

The payoff of the portfolio is the sum payoff of the short sell and long call.
The payoff of the short sell is given by

$$
\psi_{T}^{S}=-S_{T}
$$

and the payoff of the long call option is

$$
\psi_{T}^{c}=\max \left(S_{T}-K, 0\right)
$$

It follows that the portfolio pay off is

$$
\psi_{T}=\max \left(S_{T}-k, 0\right)-S_{T}
$$

Consider $S_{T}<K$

$$
\psi_{T}=S_{T}-K-S_{T}=-K
$$

and $S_{T} \geqslant K$

$$
\psi_{T}=-S_{T}
$$

Thus

$$
u_{T}= \begin{cases}-S_{T} & S_{T} \leqslant K \\ -K & S_{T}>K\end{cases}
$$

The pay off diagram for the short sell, long call and protective call are shown in the following diagram.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_21_a2ecc42d6609752f415bg-160.jpg?height=940&width=1198&top_left_y=971&top_left_x=179)

The profit of the portfolio is the sum of the shat position profit and long call profit.
The short position profit is given by

$$
\psi_{T}^{S}=S_{t}-S_{T}
$$

and the long call profit is given by

$$
\psi_{T}^{c}=\max \left(S_{T}-k, 0\right)-C\left(S_{t}, t ; k, T\right)
$$

It follows trat the portfolio profit is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \psi_{T}= S_{t}-S_{T}+\max \left(S_{T}-K, 0\right) \\
&-C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \\
& \text { for } \quad S_{T} \geqslant K \\
& \psi_{T}= S_{t}-S_{T}+S_{T}-K-C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{gathered}
=s_{t}-k-c\left(s_{t}, t ; k, T\right) \\
\text { and for } s_{T}<k \\
\psi_{T}=s_{t}-s_{T}-c\left(s_{t}, t ; k, T\right) \\
\text { thus } \\
\psi_{T}= \begin{cases}s_{t}-k-c\left(s_{t}, t ; k, T\right) & s_{T}>k \\
s_{t}-s_{T}-c\left(s_{t}, t ; k, T\right) & s_{T} \leq k\end{cases}
\end{gathered}
$$

For $S_{T} \geqslant K \quad \psi_{T}$ is constant and for $S_{T}<K \quad \psi_{T}$ is a deareasing function of $S_{T}$. It follows trat the maximum occurs for $S_{T}=0$ and is given by

$$
\psi_{T}^{+}=S_{t}-c\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \geqslant 0
$$

This inequality follows from the assumption that the price of an option is less tran the asset price.

The break suen paint of the portfolio is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
& S_{t}-S_{T}-C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)=0 \\
\Rightarrow & S_{T}^{0}=S_{t} \cdot C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

If the zero crossing occurs for $S_{T}^{\circ}<k$ it then follows that for $S_{T} \geqslant S_{T}^{0}$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& S_{t}-c\left(S_{t}, t ; k, T\right) \leqslant S_{T}^{0} \\
\Rightarrow & S_{t}-c\left(S_{t, t} ; k, T\right) \leqslant k \\
\Rightarrow & S_{t}-k-c\left(S_{t, t} ; k, T\right) \leqslant 0
\end{aligned}
$$

and thus

$$
\psi_{T}=S_{t}-K-C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \leqslant 0
$$

In summary the maximum and minimum values of the portfolio are given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \psi_{T}^{+}=S_{t}-c\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \geqslant 0 \\
& \psi_{T}^{-}=S_{t}-K-c\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \leqslant 0
\end{aligned}
$$

From these relations it follows that

$$
K \geqslant S_{t}-C\left(S_{t}, K, K, T\right)
$$

The portfolio break even occurs at

$$
S_{T}=S_{t}-C\left(S_{t}, t ; K_{1} T\right)
$$

Finally for the portfolio to be matnematically consistent

$$
C\left(S_{t}, t ; K, T\right) \geqslant S_{T}-K
$$
