$$

$$
\int_{a}^{b} f(x) d x
$$

gives

$$
d x=\frac{d g}{d y} d y
$$

and

$$
\int_{a}^{b} f(x) d x=\int_{g^{-1}(a)}^{g(b)} f(g(y)) \frac{d g}{d y} d y
$$

## Volume and Surface Area of n-Dimensional Hypersphere

In an $n$-dimensional Euclidean space a point is specified by the $n$-tuple of coordinates

$$
\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)
$$

The set of points in the space that are included in the volume of a hypersphere of radius $R$ must satisfy the equation

$$
x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2} \leqslant R^{2}
$$

A volume element is defined by

$$
d v_{n}=d x_{1} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{n}
$$

so the volume of the sphere is given by

$$
V_{n}(R)=\iint \cdots \int d x_{1} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{n}
$$

$V_{n}(R)$ must have units of $R^{n}$
so it is assumed that

$$
V_{n}(R)=C_{n} R^{n}
$$

This is consistent with the intuition obtamed from 1,2 and 3 dimensional spheres.
The swrface area of the sphere is defined by the equation

$$
x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2}=R^{2}
$$

and the integral

$$
S_{n-1}(R)=\int_{x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2}=R^{2}} \int x_{2} \cdots d x_{n}
$$

If the integral for the volume V(R) is costucted by an integral over surfaces with radius, $r$, where
then

$$
\begin{aligned}
O & \leqslant r \leqslant R \\
V_{n}(R) & =\int_{0}^{R} S_{n-1}(r) d r
\end{aligned}
$$

It follows that

$$
S_{n-1}(R)=\frac{d V_{n}}{d R}(R)=n C_{n} r^{n-1}
$$

Substitution into the previous integral gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
V_{n}(R) & =\int_{0}^{R} n C_{n} r^{n-1} d r \\
& =n C_{n} \int_{0}^{R} r^{n-1} d r \\
& =\iint_{x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2} \leq R} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{n}
\end{aligned}
$$

These are the Cartesian and
Hyper-Spherical coordinate forms
of the integral.
volume elements satisfy

$$
d x_{1} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{3}=r^{n-1} d r d \Omega_{n-1}
$$

where $d \Omega_{n-1}$ is the volume contribution from the $n-1$ angular coordinates.
for $n=2$

$$
d \Omega_{1}=d \theta
$$

and $n=3$

$$
d \Omega_{2}=\sin \theta d \theta d \varphi
$$

It follows that

$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint \cdots \int & d x_{1} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{n} \\
& =\iint \cdots \int r^{n-1} d r d \Omega_{n-1} \\
& =n C_{n} \int r^{n-1} d r
\end{aligned}
$$

thus

$$
n C_{n}=\int S \cdots S d \Omega_{n-1}
$$

This integral can be evaluated by considering the integral of the function

$$
e^{-\left(x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2}\right)}=e^{-r^{2}}
$$

Now

$$
\iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int e^{-\left(x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2}\right)} d x_{1} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{n}
$$

$=\int_{0}^{\infty} r^{n-1} e^{-r^{2}} d r \iint \cdots \int d \Omega_{n-1}$
Now the righthand side can be written as a product of $n$ identical integrals
$\iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int e^{-\left(x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2}\right)} d x_{1} d x_{1} \cdots d x_{n}$
$=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x_{1}^{2}} d x_{1} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x_{2}^{2}} d x_{2} \cdots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x_{n}^{2}} d x_{n}$
but

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2}} d x=\sqrt{\pi}
$$

thus

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int e^{-\left(x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots+x_{n}^{2}\right)} d x_{1} d x_{2} \cdots d x_{n} \\
& =\pi^{n / 2}
\end{aligned}
$$

For the lefthard side the integral over the angular coordinates

Can be evaluated since the integrand depends only on $r$. using

$$
n C_{n}=S S \cdots S d \Omega_{n-1}
$$

gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{n-1} e^{-r^{2}} d r \iint \ldots \int d \Omega_{n-1} \\
= & n C n \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{n-1} e^{-r^{2}} d r
\end{aligned}
$$

The function $\Gamma(n)$ is defined by

$$
\Gamma(n)=\int_{0}^{\infty} x^{n-1} e^{-x} d x
$$

making the change of variables

$$
x=r^{2}
$$

so

$$
\begin{aligned}
r^{n-2} & =r^{2\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)} \\
& =\left(r^{2}\right)^{\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)} \\
& =x^{\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

and

$$
d x=2 r d r \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} d x=r d r
$$

Bringing things logether gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{0}^{\infty} r^{n-1} e^{-r^{2}} d r & =\int_{0}^{\infty} r^{n-2} e^{-r^{2}} r d r \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \int_{0}^{\infty} x^{\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)} e^{-x} d x \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

This expression can be simplified further by the following property of eamma functions

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Gamma(n+1) & =\int_{0}^{\infty} x^{n} e^{-x} d x \\
& =\int_{0}^{\infty} x x^{n-1} e^{-x} d x
\end{aligned}
$$

This integral can be evaluated using integration by parts. Let

$$
u=x^{n} \quad \Rightarrow \quad d u=n x^{n-1} d x
$$

$$
d v=e^{-x} d x \Rightarrow v=-e^{-x}
$$

so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Gamma(n+1) & =-\left.x^{n} e^{-x}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}+\int_{0}^{\infty} n x^{n-1} e^{-x} d x \\
& =n \Gamma(n)
\end{aligned}
$$

using this identity/gives

$$
\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)=\frac{n}{2} \Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)
$$

An expressish for $C_{n}$ can now be determined
Bringing things together

$$
\begin{aligned}
\pi^{n / 2} & =n C_{n} \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{n-1} e^{-r^{2}} d r \\
& =n C_{n} \frac{1}{2} \Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right) \\
& =C_{n} \Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right) \\
\Rightarrow C n & =\frac{\pi^{n / 2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

and finally

$$
V_{n}(R)=\frac{\pi^{n / 2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)} R^{n}
$$

and

$$
S_{n-1}(R)=\frac{d V_{n}(R)}{d R}=\frac{n \pi^{n / 2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)} R^{n-1}
$$

So

$$
\frac{V_{n}(R)}{S_{n-1}(R)}=\frac{R}{n}
$$

To verify for $n=2$

$$
V_{2}(R)=\frac{\pi}{\Gamma(2)} R^{2}=\pi R^{2}
$$

and for $n=3$

$$
\Gamma\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)=\frac{3}{2} \Gamma\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)=\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)
$$

Now

$$
\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\int_{0}^{\infty} x^{-1 / 2} e^{-x} d x
$$

making the change of variables

$$
x=y^{2} \Rightarrow d x=2 y d y
$$

80

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\int_{0}^{\infty} y^{-1} e^{-y^{2}}(2 y) d y \\
& 2 \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-y^{2}} d y=2\left(\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}\right) \\
&=\sqrt{\pi}
\end{aligned}
$$

It follows that

$$
\Gamma\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)=\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \sqrt{\pi}=\frac{3}{4} \sqrt{\pi}
$$

so the volume of a 3 sphere is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
V_{3}(R) & =\frac{\pi^{3 / 2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)} R^{3} \\
& =\frac{\pi^{3 / 2}}{\frac{3}{4} \sqrt{\pi}} R^{3} \\
& =\frac{4}{3} \pi R^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$

It is teresting to consider a few limits Recall

$$
V_{n}(R)=\frac{\pi^{n / 2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)} R^{n}
$$

Now for $n \gg 1$ sterling's approximation gives

$$
\Gamma(n+1)=\sqrt{2 \pi n}\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^{n}
$$

So

$$
\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right) \approx \sqrt{\pi n}\left(\frac{n}{2 e}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}}
$$

Now

$$
\begin{aligned}
V_{n}(R) & =\frac{\pi^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)} R^{n} \\
& =\pi^{\frac{n}{2}} R^{n}\left[\sqrt{\pi n}\left(\frac{n}{2 e}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}}\right]^{-1} \\
& =\pi^{\frac{n}{2}} R^{n}\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi n}}\left(\frac{n}{2 e}\right)^{-\frac{n}{2}}\right] \\
& =\frac{\pi^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\sqrt{\pi}} R^{n} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\left(\frac{n}{2 e}\right)^{-\frac{n}{2}} \\
& =\frac{\pi^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\sqrt{\pi}} R^{n} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\left(\frac{2 e}{n}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}} \\
& =\frac{1}{\sqrt{n \pi}}\left(\frac{2 \pi e}{n}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}} R^{n} \\
& =\frac{1}{\sqrt{n \pi}}\left(\frac{2 \pi e R^{2}}{n}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}}
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the volume of an $n$-dimensional sphere is given by

$$
V_{n}(R)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{n \pi}}\left(\frac{2 \pi e R^{2}}{n}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}}
$$

Consider the limit $n \rightarrow \infty$ with $R$ fixed. Recall that

$$
\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty}\left(\frac{A}{n}\right)^{\frac{n}{B}}=0
$$

it follows that for a fixed radius $R$ the volume of a hypersphere vanishes as the dimension of the space goes to infinity,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} V_{n}(R) & =\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n \pi}}\left(\frac{2 \pi e R^{2}}{n}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}} \\
& =0
\end{aligned}
$$
